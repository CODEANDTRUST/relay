Day 3 after Hurricane Lila, a Cat 4 that made landfall near Cedar Key two days ago — Big Bend Florida, echoing Idalia '23 and Helene '24. Five counties without power. Cell coverage is patchy. The state has been at SERT Level 1 for 48 hours.
Maria Vidal, 47, Deputy Emergency Management Coordinator for Levy County. Came up through fire service, 12 years in EM. Hour 38 of an 18-on / 6-off rotation. Sleeping on a cot in the back room of the hardened county EOC in Bronson. Same polo as yesterday.
Diego Reyes, 32, Florida National Guard SPC. Day job: logistics for a Publix DC in Jacksonville. Drove down Sunday. Running POD-3 at Chiefland High School — water, MREs, ice, tarps — under a squad leader who reports to the FNG LNO at Maria's EOC. Diego has never heard of Maria.

	1	0530 / 0600 — Both wake. Maria walks past the "tac board" in the hallway: a magnetic whiteboard of T-cards (ICS 219) for every unit. Half the cards are wrong because the night shift didn't move them. The digital version in WebEOC is more wrong, so the wall is the source of truth. Diego gets a printed assignment sheet last updated at 1900 yesterday — which, in disaster time, is forever ago.
	2	0700 — Maria briefs the Commissioners. They want one number: how many people have we fed? The number lives in three places — the radio log from PODs 1/2/3, Red Cross ESF6 shelter feeds, and a church-group POD operating outside her chain of command. She picks "about 11,000" and writes it on a napkin so she can defend it later. (A single canonical "people served" counter in the tracker would already have paid for itself this morning.)
	3	0800 — Diego opens the gate. 90 cars in line. 7 Guardsmen, 3 county employees, and 4 unaffiliated volunteers who showed up at 0730 and signed a clipboard nobody will read. He has a magnetic count clicker his squad leader handed him in 2018. He's already lost count three times by 0830 because he keeps having to put it down to do other things.
	4	0830 — Maria opens WebEOC. Resource Requests board: 47 open items. 12 are the same generator request for POD-2, entered by different people across different shifts. She spends 20 minutes deduping by hand. (Every demo of WebEOC, Crisis Track, and Veoci treats duplicates as a UX edge case. In a real activation it's the modal entry pattern.)
	5	1000 — Diego radios in. Cars served 280, water 60%, MREs 70%, ice gone (it was gone by 0930). He requests 4 more pallets of water. The ESF7 LNO at the EOC writes it on a Post-It: "I'll put it in WebEOC." Diego doesn't know what WebEOC is. He's been told the word four times in 48 hours; it has not made it into his working vocabulary because nothing about WebEOC is for him.
	6	1100 — A runner brings Maria a paper ICS 213 from Diego, dated 0945, hand-delivered via the last Guard truck. "Water 50% gone by 1400 ETA. Lines 90 min. Request 4 pallets." This is the first she's heard. The radio call he made at 1000 went into a different shift's logs and won't surface until evening turnover. (The same request now exists across three media — radio, paper, a Post-It — and is not in any system Maria can search.)
	7	1230 — The Georgia church group. A 60-person bus from a church in Albany pulls up at Maria's EOC with two box trucks of clothes, baby food, and dog kibble. Nobody asked for clothes. She doesn't have a place to put them, anyone to manage them, or permission to turn them away. She hands them a paper map to a parking lot near POD-2 and walks away knowing those clothes will sit in the rain for two weeks. (Every disaster surfaces the "donation surge" and no existing tool models it. The tracker needs a "what we explicitly DON'T need" channel, broadcast outward.)
	8	1400 — The bus arrives at Diego's gate. He doesn't have permission to accept or refuse. He sends them to the Walmart parking lot across the street, where they hand clothes out for free. Half his line walks over. He loses 30 minutes of throughput. He has no way to tell Maria this happened.
	9	1500 — Maria's ToughBooks go dark. Three sub-county PODs stop reporting because their LTE pucks died. Sprint is silent, the state IT line says "try Verizon." She tells her LNO to "call them on the cell every hour and write it down." (Every existing tool assumes always-on internet. Field reality is intermittent LTE, often via a personal phone with 12% battery and someone else's borrowed login.)
	10	1730 — Diego locks up. Final tally on his clipboard: 612 cars, ~1,800 people fed, 30 turned away, 4 medical referrals (one a dialysis patient he gave two bottles of water and a phone number she couldn't call). Paper goes to his squad leader, then to a county runner, then to a reservist who types it into WebEOC around 2200. Diego's 4-pallet water request from 1000 arrives at 1830, past lockup. Maria sees the 612 cars in her 0700 brief the next morning. Diego is asleep on a cot at the FNG armory by 1930, phone at 12%, plugged into the truck.
	11	1830 — Maria's PIO problem. A Tampa Bay Times reporter calls. The PIO is at POD-2 because Tier 1 wanted "PIO presence" with the public. Maria takes it in the hallway. The reporter asks: how many supplies, how many people, when will power be back. She doesn't know any of the three. (This is the moment the tracker stops being internal tooling and becomes a defensible source of truth — the controller needs to point at one screen and say "those numbers, right now.")
	12	2015 — Maria drives home. House has no power. Granola bar over the sink, showers in the dark, alarm for 0500. Tomorrow they're activating two more PODs.

What the software gap actually is
The thing connecting Maria and Diego today is four media — radio, Post-Its, carbon-copy ICS 213s, and a county-issued ToughBook with no service. The first three are surprisingly good: they survive power outages, need no login, leave a paper trail. The fourth is the only one supposed to scale, and it's the one that consistently fails.
A demo-scale tracker that splits the difference — Diego types on his own phone (one-tap quantity bumps, no login, optimistic local writes that sync when LTE returns), Maria reads a wall (one screen, one truth, duplicates visible) — would have saved that 4-pallet request a five-hour delay. It wouldn't have saved the dialysis patient. The point isn't to digitize everything; it's to make the next-most-critical thing on Maria's wall always be current.
Five design choices that fall directly out of this day:
	•	No login on the field side. Diego is using a borrowed phone half the time. Auth = a 6-digit POD code printed on his assignment sheet, that's it.
	•	One-tap quantity bumps, no freeform. "Water -10%", "Ice empty", "Open", "Closed." Anything that requires typing won't get typed.
	•	Duplicates are the default, not the edge case. The wall surfaces "5 sites have all reported low water in the last 30 min" as one card, not five.
	•	A "don't send" channel. "POD-3 has plenty of clothes. POD-2 needs water." Public-facing so the church bus stops a county away.
	•	Last-known-good is a first-class state. When Diego's LTE dies at 1530, his card on Maria's wall stays visible with a "last updated 1000" timestamp, not vanishes. The cost of disappearing is higher than the cost of being slightly stale
